Earthquake Preparedness Guide

Earthquakes are one of the most unpredictable and potentially destructive natural disasters. Unlike hurricanes or floods, earthquakes strike without warning, making advance preparation essential for minimizing casualties and property damage.

What to Do Before an Earthquake

Create an emergency plan for your household. Identify safe spots in each room, such as under sturdy tables or against interior walls away from windows. Practice drop, cover, and hold on drills so that all household members respond automatically during shaking.

Assemble an emergency supply kit containing water (one gallon per person per day for at least 72 hours), non-perishable food, a first aid kit, flashlights, batteries, a battery-powered radio, dust masks, plastic sheeting, and important documents in waterproof containers.

Secure heavy furniture, appliances, and tall bookshelves to walls using furniture straps. Store heavy items on lower shelves. Know how to shut off gas, water, and electricity at the main switches, and keep the necessary tools nearby.

What to Do During an Earthquake

If you are indoors, drop to your hands and knees, take cover under a sturdy desk or table, and hold on until shaking stops. If no shelter is nearby, cover your head and neck with your arms. Stay away from windows, outside doors, and walls. Do not use elevators.

If you are outdoors, move away from buildings, streetlights, and utility wires. Once in the open, drop to the ground and stay there until shaking stops. If you are in a moving vehicle, pull over to a clear location, stop, and stay inside with your seatbelt fastened.

What to Do After an Earthquake

Expect aftershocks. These secondary shaking events can occur minutes, days, or even weeks after the main earthquake. Aftershocks can cause further damage to weakened structures.

Check yourself and others for injuries. Provide first aid as needed. Do not attempt to move seriously injured people unless they are in immediate danger. Check your home for damage. If it appears unsafe to occupy, evacuate and do not return until authorities declare it safe.

If you smell gas, open windows and leave the building immediately. Do not use electrical switches, appliances, or open flames, as these can ignite gas. Report the leak to the gas company from a safe location.

Listen to a battery-operated radio for emergency information and instructions from local authorities. Use the telephone only for serious emergencies to keep lines available.

Structural Safety and Building Resilience

Buildings constructed before modern seismic codes are at greater risk of damage. Older unreinforced masonry structures, soft-story apartment buildings, and homes with cripple walls are especially vulnerable to earthquake forces.

Seismic retrofitting involves strengthening an existing structure to make it more resistant to seismic activity. Homeowners can have their foundations inspected and bolted to the frame, cripple walls braced, and water heaters strapped. These measures significantly reduce the likelihood of major structural damage.

Local governments and building departments often maintain resources about seismic hazard zones and can provide guidance on obtaining permits for retrofit work. Some jurisdictions offer low-interest loans or rebates for homeowners who complete seismic improvements.

Emergency Response Planning for Communities

Emergency managers play a critical role in coordinating response efforts after a major seismic event. Pre-event planning includes maintaining updated contact lists, establishing clear chains of command, and pre-positioning emergency supplies and equipment in strategic locations throughout the community.

Community resilience depends on trained volunteers. Programs like Community Emergency Response Team (CERT) train residents to assist with basic first aid, light search and rescue, and fire suppression in the immediate aftermath of a disaster, before professional responders can arrive.

Mutual aid agreements between neighboring jurisdictions allow communities to share resources and personnel during a large-scale emergency. These agreements should be established and tested through exercises before a disaster occurs.

Tsunami Awareness for Coastal Regions

Earthquakes occurring under the ocean or near coastlines can generate tsunamis. If you are in a coastal area and experience strong ground shaking, treat it as a natural warning and move immediately to high ground or inland without waiting for an official warning.

Tsunami warning systems use seismic sensors and ocean buoys to detect and report tsunami waves. When warnings are issued, follow evacuation routes and move quickly to designated safe zones. Do not return to coastal areas until authorities give the all-clear.

Know the difference between a tsunami watch and a tsunami warning. A watch means conditions are favorable for a tsunami to form; a warning means a tsunami has been detected and is potentially destructive.

Schools, Hospitals, and Critical Facilities

Schools are required in many states to conduct regular earthquake drills. Teachers and staff should be trained in drop-cover-hold-on procedures, first aid, and evacuation protocols. After a significant earthquake, schools should inspect buildings before allowing re-entry.

Hospitals and emergency facilities must remain operational during and after disasters. Hospital administrators should evaluate their buildings for structural vulnerabilities and retrofit as needed. Backup generators, water supplies, and medical stockpiles are essential for continued operations.

Critical infrastructure including water treatment plants, communication towers, and transportation networks must be hardened against seismic damage to support community recovery efforts.

Psychological Impacts and Recovery

The psychological impact of earthquakes should not be underestimated. Fear, anxiety, and post-traumatic stress are common reactions, especially after major events or repeated aftershocks. Community mental health resources should be integrated into disaster response planning.

Recovery from a major earthquake can take years. Long-term recovery plans should address housing, economic revitalization, infrastructure repair, and community well-being. Strong community networks and social cohesion are among the most important factors in successful earthquake recovery.
